<p><i><b>Donuts</b></i> is the second <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" class="mw-redirect" title="Studio album">studio album</a> by the American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_production" title="Hip hop production">hip hop producer</a> <a href="/wiki/J_Dilla" title="J Dilla">J Dilla</a>, released on February 7, 2006 by <a href="/wiki/Stones_Throw_Records" title="Stones Throw Records">Stones Throw Records</a>. It was released on the day of his 32nd birthday and three days before his death, thus making it the final album released during J Dilla's lifetime.
</p><p>On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, <i>Donuts</i> has received "universal acclaim" from critics, based on an aggregate score of 84/100 from 15 reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-metacritic_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metacritic-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" class="mw-redirect" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork</a> placed the album at number 38 on their list of the top 50 albums of 2006<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and at number 66 on their list of the top 200 albums of the 2000s.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donuts_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2002, J Dilla had been diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Thrombotic_thrombocytopenic_purpura" title="Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura">thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura</a>, an incurable disease of the blood, while also battling <a href="/wiki/Systemic_lupus_erythematosus" title="Systemic lupus erythematosus">lupus</a>, which had been diagnosed a year previously. According to close friend and fellow producer <a href="/wiki/Karriem_Riggins" title="Karriem Riggins">Karriem Riggins</a>, the impetus for <i>Donuts</i> came during an extended hospital stay in the summer of 2005.
</p><p>In the December 2006 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fader" title="The Fader">The Fader</a></i>, J Dilla's mother, Maureen Yancey, a former <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a> singer, spoke of watching her son's daily routine during the making of <i>Donuts</i>:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>I knew he was working on a series of beat CDs before he came to Los Angeles. <i>Donuts</i> was a special project that he hadn't named yet. This was the tail end of his "Dill Withers" phase, while he was living in <a href="/wiki/Clinton_Township,_Macomb_County,_Michigan" title="Clinton Township, Macomb County, Michigan">Clinton Township</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>. You see, musically he went into different phases. He'd start on a project, go back, go buy more records and then go back to working on the project again.
</p><p>I saw him all day, everyday. I would go there for breakfast, go back to Detroit to check on the daycare business I was running, and then back to his house for lunch and dinner. He was on a special diet and he was a funny eater anyway. He had to take 15 different medications, we would split them up between meals, and every other day we would binge on a brownie sundae from Big Boys. That was his treat.
</p><p>I didn't know about the actual album <i>Donuts</i> until I came to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> to stay indefinitely. I got a glimpse of the music during one of the hospital stays, around his 31st birthday, when [friend and producer] House Shoes came out from Detroit to visit him. I would sneak in and listen to the work in progress while he was in <a href="/wiki/Dialysis" title="Dialysis">dialysis</a>. He got furious when he found out I was listening to his music! He didn't want me to listen to anything until it was a finished product.
</p><p>He was working in the hospital. He tried to go over each beat and make sure that it was something different and make sure that there was nothing that he wanted to change. "Lightworks", oh yes, that was something! That's one of the special ones. It was so different. It blended classical music (way out there classical), commercial and <a href="/wiki/Underground_hip_hop" title="Underground hip hop">underground</a> at the same time.<sup id="cite_ref-fader_blog_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fader_blog-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donuts_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Donuts</i> is an <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_hip_hop" class="mw-redirect" title="Instrumental hip hop">instrumental hip hop</a> album;<sup id="cite_ref-Rebirth_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebirth-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> the only lyrics on it are short phrases and gasps taken from various records.<sup id="cite_ref-popmatters_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-popmatters-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <i>Donuts</i> contains 31 tracks, which was J Dilla's age at the time of recording.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Most songs are quite short, running at lengths of 1–1.5 minutes each,<sup id="cite_ref-rs_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rs-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and vary in style and tone.<sup id="cite_ref-popmatters_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-popmatters-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Clash_(magazine)" title="Clash (magazine)">Clash</a></i> called the album "a conversation between two completely different producers".<sup id="cite_ref-clash_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clash-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The original <a href="/wiki/Press_release" title="Press release">press release</a> for the album compared it to scanning radio stations in an unfamiliar city.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFerguson20143_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFerguson20143-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The track order is also unusual: the album begins with an outro and ends with the intro.<sup id="cite_ref-donuts10_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-donuts10-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-popmatters_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-popmatters-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> According to Collin Robinson of <i><a href="/wiki/Stereogum" title="Stereogum">Stereogum</a></i>, "it's almost too perfect a metaphor for Dilla's otherworldly ability to flip the utter shit out of anything he sampled".<sup id="cite_ref-donuts10_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-donuts10-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The ending of the final track flows right into the beginning of the first one,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFerguson201499_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFerguson201499-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> forming an infinite loop,<sup id="cite_ref-popmatters100_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-popmatters100-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and alluding to <a href="/wiki/Donuts" class="mw-redirect" title="Donuts">donuts</a>' circular form.<sup id="cite_ref-donuts10_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-donuts10-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-toro_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toro-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donuts_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2005, J Dilla underwent treatment at <a href="/wiki/Cedars-Sinai_Medical_Center" title="Cedars-Sinai Medical Center">Cedars-Sinai Medical Center</a> for complications brought on by TTP and a form of <a href="/wiki/Systemic_lupus_erythematosus" title="Systemic lupus erythematosus">lupus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFerguson20142_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFerguson20142-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> While in hospital, he worked on two albums: <i>Donuts</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shining_(J_Dilla_album)" title="The Shining (J Dilla album)">The Shining</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> 29 out of 31 tracks from <i>Donuts</i> were recorded in hospital,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> using a <a href="/wiki/Roland_SP-303" class="mw-redirect" title="Roland SP-303">Boss SP-303</a> sampler and a small 45 <a href="/wiki/Phonograph" title="Phonograph">record player</a> his friends brought him.<sup id="cite_ref-p4k_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p4k-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Records his mother and friends would bring were used as the source of the samples for the album.<sup id="cite_ref-popmatters_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-popmatters-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> She recalled it in the <i>Crate Diggers</i> documentary:<sup id="cite_ref-vice_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vice-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> "When I took the crate up, and he looked through it, I think out of a whole milk crate full of 45s, I think he might have taken a dozen out of there and set them aside. He said 'you can take that back to the house'. He said 'none of that's good'."
</p><p>Throughout the year his condition worsened. His legs swelled, making it difficult to walk. At times his hands swelled so much he could barely move them. If the pain was too intense, his mother would massage his fingertips, so he could continue working on the album. Sometimes he'd wake up in the middle of the night and ask his mother to move him from his bed to the instruments. According to Kelley L. Carter of <i><a href="/wiki/Detroit_Free_Press" title="Detroit Free Press">Detroit Free Press</a></i>, J Dilla told his doctor he was proud of the work, and that all he wanted to do was to finish the album.<sup id="cite_ref-dfp_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dfp-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While working on the album, Dilla didn't allow anyone to listen to the unfinished version.<sup id="cite_ref-vice_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vice-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> He got furious when he found out his mother listened to it while he was in <a href="/wiki/Dialysis" title="Dialysis">dialysis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fader_blog_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fader_blog-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_promotion">Release and promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donuts_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Release and promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Donuts</i> was ready to be released by October 2005, but according to <a href="/wiki/Stones_Throw" class="mw-redirect" title="Stones Throw">Stones Throw</a>, their distributor, <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a>, "didn't think a weird, difficult instrumental album by an underground producer would move the projected 10,000 copies", since Dilla's previous album, <i><a href="/wiki/Champion_Sound" title="Champion Sound">Champion Sound</a></i>, failed to achieve commercial success.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFerguson201474_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFerguson201474-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Later the label came to an understanding with the distributor and the album was set for release in early February 2006, along with a bonus single "Signs".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFerguson201476_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFerguson201476-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Donuts</i> was released on February 7, 2006, J Dilla's 32nd birthday.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> To celebrate this, his friends, <a href="/wiki/Madlib" title="Madlib">Madlib</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peanut_Butter_Wolf" title="Peanut Butter Wolf">Peanut Butter Wolf</a>, Egon, and <a href="/wiki/J_Rocc" class="mw-redirect" title="J Rocc">J Rocc</a>, stopped by his house. However, they weren't prepared for what they saw: J Dilla, who used to be energetic despite his health condition, now was mumbling and gesturing weakly.<sup id="cite_ref-dfp_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dfp-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFerguson201477_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFerguson201477-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Three days later, on February 10, 2006, he died at his home in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. According to his mother, the cause was <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrest" title="Cardiac arrest">cardiac arrest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Donuts:_J_Rocc.27s_Picks"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Donuts:_J_Rocc's_Picks">Donuts: J Rocc's Picks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donuts_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Donuts: J Rocc&#039;s Picks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Donuts_EP:_J._Rocc%27s_Picks" title="Donuts EP: J. Rocc&#39;s Picks">Donuts EP: J. Rocc's Picks</a></div>
<p>To promote the album, Stones Throw, in association with <a href="/wiki/Guitar_Center" title="Guitar Center">Guitar Center</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adult_Swim" title="Adult Swim">Adult Swim</a>, released a limited edition <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a> called <i>Donuts EP: J. Rocc's Picks</i>. The EP contained five extended versions of <i>Donuts</i> instrumentals and the bonus track, "Signs". Copies of the EP were given away on <a href="/wiki/Winter_Music_Conference" title="Winter Music Conference">Winter Music Conference</a> (WMC) 2006 and <a href="/wiki/South_by_Southwest" title="South by Southwest">South by Southwest</a> (SXSW) 2006. The label later started selling <a href="/wiki/Digital_distribution" title="Digital distribution">digital versions</a> of the EP on their official site.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rereleases">Rereleases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donuts_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rereleases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 2013, the album was rereleased as a <a href="/wiki/Box_set" title="Box set">box set</a>. Apart from seven <a href="/wiki/7%22" class="mw-redirect" title="7&quot;">7-inch vinyl records</a> it contained a bonus 7-inch with tracks "Signs" and "Sniper Elite &amp; Murder Goons", featuring <a href="/wiki/MF_Doom" title="MF Doom">MF Doom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ghostface_Killah" title="Ghostface Killah">Ghostface Killah</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> A number of music journalists criticized the box set, stating that the album should be listened as a whole and shouldn't be split.<sup id="cite_ref-toro_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toro-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-p4k_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p4k-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 27, 2014, <i>Donuts</i> was released on <a href="/wiki/Compact_cassette" class="mw-redirect" title="Compact cassette">compact cassettes</a>, as a part of <a href="/wiki/Cassette_Store_Day" title="Cassette Store Day">Cassette Store Day</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2016, on <i>Donuts'</i>s 10th anniversary, LP version of the album was rereleased. It included the original cover art with Jeff Jank's drawing on it, new drawing on the back, and liner notes by Jordan Ferguson, containing an excerpt from his book <i>Donuts</i> from <a href="/wiki/33%E2%85%93" title="33⅓">33⅓ series</a> about the making of the album.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donuts_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dilla's death, three days after the album's release, was widely mourned by the hip hop community, including all those who worked with him in the past and the years closer to his death, especially <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>'s hip-hop community (which included rapper <a href="/wiki/Proof_(rapper)" title="Proof (rapper)">Proof</a>, a friend and associate of Dilla's, who also died 2 months later on April 11).
</p><p>In regards to the name, "Donuts," The New York Times published an article on Dilla's death, on February 14, 2006, saying, "The record company issued a brief note about the title: 'Easy explanation. Dilla likes donuts.' Yesterday his mother managed a chuckle when she confirmed that fact. 'I just bought two dozen a week ago,' she said."<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p><i>Donuts</i> received "universal acclaim" from music critics. The album holds a score of 84 out of 100 on the review aggregate site <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, indicating "universal acclaim".<sup id="cite_ref-metacritic_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metacritic-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Will Dukes of <i><a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_(website)" title="Pitchfork (website)">Pitchfork</a></i> wrote that <i>Donuts</i> showcases Dilla paying homage to "the selfsame sounds he's modernized", and in that sense, the album "is pure postmodern art—which was hip-hop's aim in the first place."<sup id="cite_ref-Dukes_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dukes-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/PopMatters" title="PopMatters">PopMatters</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span> Michael Frauenhofer described <i>Donuts</i> as an "album of explosions and restraint, of precisely crafted balances and absurd breakdowns, of the senselessly affecting juxtaposition of the most powerful of dreams."<sup id="cite_ref-popmatters_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-popmatters-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Rabin" title="Nathan Rabin">Nathan Rabin</a> noted Dilla's "ability to twist and contort samples into unrecognizable new forms" and concluded that "as an album from one of rap's most revered producers on one of hip-hop's most respected labels, <i>Donuts</i> would qualify as a fairly major release under any circumstances, but J Dilla's recent death lends it additional significance and gravity."<sup id="cite_ref-Club_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Club-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Andy Kellman of <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> wrote that <i>Donuts</i> "has a resonance deeper than anyone could've hoped for or even imagined" given Dilla's passing shortly after its release, and ultimately "just might be the one release that best reflects his personality".<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Giving it a three-star honorable mention rating in his review for <a href="/wiki/MSN_Music" title="MSN Music">MSN Music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> called <i>Donuts</i> "more about moments than flow, which is strange when you think about it".<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a 2007 guest column for <i>Pitchfork</i>, <a href="/wiki/Panda_Bear_(musician)" title="Panda Bear (musician)">Panda Bear</a> of <a href="/wiki/Animal_Collective" title="Animal Collective">Animal Collective</a> stated that <i>Donuts</i> was "By far the album I've listened to most over the past year, and I feel like almost any of the songs off there I could say is my favorite."<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Online music service <a href="/wiki/Rhapsody_(online_music_service)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhapsody (online music service)">Rhapsody</a> ranked the album at number three on its "Hip-Hop's Best Albums of the Decade" list.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> It ranked number nine on <i><a href="/wiki/Clash_(magazine)" title="Clash (magazine)">Clash</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s Essential 50 countdown in April 2009,<sup id="cite_ref-clash_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clash-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and the magazine later wrote that its "legacy is undeniable".<sup id="cite_ref-Clash_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clash-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In a 2012 review of the <i>Donuts</i> 45 box set, <i>Pitchfork</i> accorded the album a revised 10/10 rating, with critic Nate Patrin writing: "It's a widely praised favorite for so many people, and yet there's something about <i>Donuts</i> that feels like such an intensely personal statement".<sup id="cite_ref-p4k_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p4k-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Q_(magazine)" title="Q (magazine)">Q</a></i>, in 2017, called it a "tour de force in postmodern beatmaking".<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donuts_(album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The tracks "One for Ghost" and "Hi" were used in <a href="/wiki/Ghostface_Killah" title="Ghostface Killah">Ghostface Killah</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Fishscale" title="Fishscale">Fishscale</a></i>, under the names "Whip You With a Strap" and "Beauty Jackson", respectively. Ghostface Killah also used "Geek Down" for the song "Murda Goons",<sup id="cite_ref-Vocaldonut_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vocaldonut-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> released on his <i><a href="/wiki/Hidden_Darts:_Special_Edition" title="Hidden Darts: Special Edition">Hidden Darts: Special Edition</a></i> album. <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rah_Digga" title="Rah Digga">Rah Digga</a> used "Gobstopper" and "Last Donut of the Night" as beats for "Just Another Day at the Range" and "Best That Ever Did It." "Workinonit" was used by <a href="/wiki/The_Roots" title="The Roots">The Roots</a> for a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Saigon_(rapper)" title="Saigon (rapper)">Saigon</a> for the album <i>Game Theory</i>, however it was not included out of respect for Dilla's passing.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The verse from Saigon can be heard on his mixtape <i>Return of the Yardfather</i>.  J Dilla's posthumously released album <i>The Shining</i>, also released with new verses on Common's <i>Finding Forever</i>, uses a re-edited version of "Bye."<sup id="cite_ref-Vocaldonut_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vocaldonut-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The aforementioned tracks were, for the most part, recorded or planned during Dilla's lifetime.  After Dilla's passing, The Roots used "Time: The Donut of the Heart" for their J Dilla tribute "Can't Stop This" on the album <i><a href="/wiki/Game_Theory_(album)" title="Game Theory (album)">Game Theory</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Contentedbest_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Contentedbest-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>  In 2005, the track "Mash" was rapped over by <a href="/wiki/MF_Doom" title="MF Doom">MF DOOM</a> and Guilty Simpson on the track "Mash's Revenge", which appears on the Stones Throw compilation "B-Ball Zombie War". DOOM also used "Anti-American Graffiti" which appeared on the Dilla Ghost Doom release <i><a href="/wiki/Sniperlite" title="Sniperlite">Sniperlite</a></i>, under the track name "Sniper Elite". DOOM later used "Lightworks" on a track of the same name on his album <i><a href="/wiki/Born_Like_This" title="Born Like This">Born Like This</a></i>. "Lightworks" was also used for the "B-Ball Zombie War" track "Lightworking," which features <a href="/wiki/Talib_Kweli" title="Talib Kweli">Talib Kweli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Q-Tip_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Q-Tip (rapper)">Q-Tip</a>. <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a> added a verse to Q-Tip and Talib Kweli's on "Lightworks" and included it in his 2007 mixtape <i>Dillagence</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Vocaldonut_44-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vocaldonut-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cartoon_Network" title="Cartoon Network">Cartoon Network</a> has used many of the album's tracks as <a href="/wiki/Bumper_music" title="Bumper music">bumper music</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Adult_Swim" title="Adult Swim">Adult Swim</a> programming block. Adult Swim, which has been in a partnership with Stones Throw records, cited the track "Stepson of the Clapper" as their addiction.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many other rappers and hip hop artists have started to use various beats from <i>Donuts</i>. <a href="/wiki/Termanology" title="Termanology">Termanology</a> also recorded a track titled, "Only One Can Win" using J Dilla's track "Two Can Win." The song is a tale about a man choosing between rap and a woman.  He pays respect to Dilla in the beginning of the song. <a href="/wiki/Talib_Kweli" title="Talib Kweli">Talib Kweli</a> has used "Bye" on a track called "I Feel You" from the 2006 mixtape <i>Blacksmith: The Movement</i><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> and "Dilla Says Go" on a track called "Kweli Says Go" from the mixtape with <a href="/wiki/Clinton_Sparks" title="Clinton Sparks">Clinton Sparks</a> "Get Familiar". Rapper <a href="/wiki/Big_Pooh" title="Big Pooh">Big Pooh</a> had used "Gobstopper" for a track titled "Plastic Cups",<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> and he also used "One Eleven" for a track with the same name featuring O-Dash on a mixtape with Mick Boogie. <a href="/wiki/Drake_(entertainer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Drake (entertainer)">Drake</a> used "Time: The Donut of the Heart" in a song called "Where to Now" on his mixtape <i>Comeback Season</i> (2007).<sup id="cite_ref-Contentedbest_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Contentedbest-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Hamilton_(rapper)" title="Charles Hamilton (rapper)">Charles Hamilton</a> created a mixtape titled <i>And Then They Played Dilla</i> rapping over tracks from <i>Donuts</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> He also created a sequel, which is named "And Then They Played Dilla 2".
</p><p>Rapper  <a href="/wiki/Skyzoo" title="Skyzoo">Skyzoo</a> has recorded tribute tracks using "Two Can Win" and "Last Donut," among others. <a href="/wiki/Jay_Electronica" title="Jay Electronica">Jay Electronica</a> used "Gobstopper" for his track "Abracadabra" and several other Dilla beats for various tracks of his <i>Victory</i> mixtape.<sup id="cite_ref-donuts10_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-donuts10-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/XV_(rapper)" title="XV (rapper)">XV</a> released <i>Thanks For The Donuts</i>, a tribute EP using J Dilla beats, on February 7, 2011 (Dilla's birthday as well as the fifth anniversary of <i>Donuts</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Big_Sean" title="Big Sean">Big Sean</a> has also released freestyle which uses the beat for "(Only) Two Can Win", and uses the same title.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a> released "The Season" on October 30, 2014 which uses "Gobstopper" as the backdrop for his track.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> J Dilla is listed as the producer. <a href="/wiki/Lupe_Fiasco" title="Lupe Fiasco">Lupe Fiasco</a> used "The Diff'rence" on the track "Of" from his August 29, 2015 mixtape "Pharaoh Heights".<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017, <a href="/wiki/Dave_Chappelle" title="Dave Chappelle">Dave Chappelle</a> used "Workinonit" as the theme music for his two Netflix stand-up specials.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
